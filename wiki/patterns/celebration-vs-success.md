# Celebration vs. success message

**Definition:** Two distinct response types for positive outcomes. Most completed tasks warrant a neutral success message. Only genuine customer milestones warrant celebration.

**The rule:** Celebrate what the *customer* accomplished, not what *we* did or what benefits *us*.

---

## When to celebrate (use sparingly)

- Customer reached a self-set goal (paid off debt, hit savings target)
- Major milestone (first payroll run, taxes e-filed, business anniversary)
- Outcome that matters to them personally (refund deposited, loan approved)
- Payoff from a habit change (more savings, books closed on time)
- Promised product outcome delivered (first payment received and deposited)

**Good celebration copy:**
- "Monique, you paid off your loan! Fantastic work!"
- "You did it! You just e-filed your taxes."
- "You crushed it! Congrats on paying down your $6,420 credit card debt."
- "Time to celebrate! You accomplished your savings goal!"

---

## When to use a success message instead

- Completed a routine or recurring task
- Turned on a preference or setting
- Made incremental progress toward a goal
- Submitted a request (result not yet confirmed)

**Good success message copy:**
- Friendly, neutral, confirms completion
- Doesn't perform enthusiasm

---

## Things to avoid in celebration copy

- "Right on! You've signed in successfully." (signing in is not a milestone)
- "Woo hoo! You sent your 57th invoice." (routine task)
- "Nice job. You made it through Step 2!" (patronizing)
- "You started your taxes—time for a happy dance!" (premature)
- "Get ready to celebrate. You might have a refund!" (speculative)

---

## Sanity checks before adding a celebration

1. How often does this moment occur? If daily/weekly, a celebration cheapens the experience.
2. Could this celebration backfire? (E.g., a tax break triggered by a medical hardship — not celebratory.)
3. Are there nearby celebrations in the same flow? Stacking celebrations undermines all of them.
4. Would a simple success message do the job?
