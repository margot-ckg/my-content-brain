# Emotional design

**Definition:** Design that evokes appropriate emotions to build meaningful relationships with customers — through empathy, reassurance, and occasional delight — without manipulation, overpromising, or being saccharine.

**When to use:**
- Onboarding moments where customers need reassurance
- Error states where customers feel stuck or anxious
- Milestone moments where customers deserve acknowledgment
- Any moment where the customer's emotional state is visible from context

**When NOT to use:**
- Transactional confirmations where functional clarity matters more
- When the emotional framing would feel forced or condescending
- To cover up a product failure with warm language

**Core moves:**
1. **Empathize first.** Acknowledge what the customer might be feeling before moving to action.
   - "We know change can be hard. We're here for you." ✓
   - "Great news! Your payroll system is modernizing." ✗ (assumes emotion)
2. **Reassure with specifics.** Vague warmth feels hollow. Specific reassurance feels real.
   - "Your cart is still saved." ✓
   - "Don't worry, we got you covered." ✗ (vague, can backfire in serious situations)
3. **Let customers own the outcome.** Customer benefits, not product features.
   - "We can help you grow your small business—and we'll be with you every step of the way." ✓
   - "Have you tried this amazing new feature?" ✗ (about us, not them)

**Variations:**
- **Empathy + next step** (error recovery): name the emotion, then offer the action
- **Acknowledgment only** (empty states): meet customers where they are without pushing forward
- **Delight** (milestone): celebrate a genuine customer win — see [[celebration-vs-success]]
