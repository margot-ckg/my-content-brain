## Foundations: Emotional Design

Designs that evoke emotions build meaningful relationships with customers. We empathize, we reassure, and we sometimes surprise and delight — without overpromising, manipulation, or being saccharine.

### Put customer benefits above business goals
Focus on building a relationship, not selling. Emphasize customer benefits over business goals.

- DO: "We can help you grow your small business—and we'll be with you every step of the way"
- DON'T: "Have you tried this amazing new feature from our world-class product? Only an extra $35/mo"

### Watch your tone
Use a tone that inspires or accommodates customers' emotions. Respect their moods; don't tell them how to feel.

- DO: "We know change can be hard. We're here for you." (acknowledges concern)
- DON'T: "Great news! Your payroll system is modernizing." (assumes change is great news)
- DO: "We simplify every part of getting paid, so you can focus on what matters." (customer benefit)
- DON'T: "You're going to love our payroll solution!" (telling them how to feel)

**On "Don't worry":** In low-stakes situations it can reassure. In serious situations, avoid it — it can cause unnecessary worry. If you remove it and the content still works, you don't need it.

### Beware of superlatives
Superlatives (most, best, top, fastest, ultimate, premier, most-trusted, world-class) diminish trust and sound desperate.

- Rule: If the opposite is something we'd never say, you're using hype.
- "Good" often works better than "great."
- Facts beat superlatives. Back up bold claims with data and cite the source.

- DO: "We'll take good care of your team"
- DON'T: "We'll take great care of your team" / "The best tools from the get-go"

### Make the most of microcopy
Don't miss small content moments (under 20 seconds to read). Human-sounding voice with an opportunity to discover is compelling.

- DO: "We use cookies for sign in and checkout. Learn more" (but "Learn" can sound like a burden)
- Personalize when customers are signed in — show you know who they are.

## Foundations: Content Testing

Testing content gives confidence in design decisions. Any test, however small, teaches something.

### Know what you want to learn
Before testing, articulate: What do I want to learn? How will the outcome benefit the customer? How will I measure success?

### Go broad to learn big
Don't just tweak a few words. Frame two or more different approaches. Test boldly — compare different concepts, not just rephrased sentences.

### Quantitative vs. qualitative
- **Quantitative:** Statistical data, understand "how" users act. Good for validating solutions at scale.
- **Qualitative:** Interviews, text, audio, video. Good for understanding "why" behind actions, usability/comprehension.

### Write a research plan
Goals, target customer, hypothesis, script. Share with partners (product designers, PMs) in advance.

### Types of tests
- Customer interviews
- Card sorting (for menu items or labels)
- A/B test (once you have options from qualitative insights)
- Money test (understanding which benefits resonate)
- Highlighting test (when tone is off)

## Foundations: Writing Small

Less is (almost always) more. Customers come to products to accomplish tasks, not get bogged down in words.

### Keep it short
- Make it scannable — most people skim
- Use less content — fewer words = lighter cognitive load
- Keep it simple — complicated page looks intimidating
- Focus on what's useful
- Guide with structure — hierarchy, bullets, visual space

### Take a little off the top
Headlines should be tight and specific. Convey the gist quickly. Example: "Get expert help with Full Service" beats a 15-word version.

### Prioritize what customers need
Front-load key messages. For modals (often dismissed quickly), the CTA might do most of the informational work — make it clear and value-packed.

### Use progressive disclosure
Break long workflows into bite-sized pieces. Helps customers focus, prevents overwhelm, lowers cognitive load. Especially important for neurodivergent users (ADHD, anxiety).

Methods:
- Links to more details
- Accordion components
- Conditional fields (show follow-up fields based on user selection)
- Step-throughs with logical clusters

### Break it up
Use bullets to break large chunks of text. Use content hierarchy to help readers find details quickly.

## Voice and Tone: QuickBooks

**Brand character:** The proactive champion. Champion of those who dare to dream.

### Voice principles
**Warm:** Friendly, heard, supported, at ease. Engaged and caring. Empathetic but not sappy. Patient and approachable.

**Confident:** Focused, purposeful, self-assured but not full of ourselves. Energetic but not over the top. Smart but not smarty-pants.

**Genuine:** Trustworthy. Sincere and candid. Straightforward but not blunt. Say what we mean, don't sugarcoat.

Examples:
- DO: "Hi" or "Hey there" / "Thanks" / "You need to" / "Heads up"
- DON'T: "Greetings or Dear" / "Thank you or Salutations" / "You're required to" / "Warning"

### Tone
Voice is consistent; tone adapts to the customer's situation.

Spectrum from emotional → functional:
- Emotional: headers, email subject lines, body content
- Kind of emotional: subheads, email preheaders, help content
- Kind of functional: error messages, bulleted lists, inline messages
- Functional: tooltips, CTAs, toast messages

### Content strategy principles
1. **It's about them, not us** — don't toot our own horn
2. **Speak their language** — human-to-human, everyday words
3. **Focus on the payoff** — explain why it matters, not just what to do
4. **Keep it simple** — only use words that are necessary
5. **Delight when it's right** — make customers smile at the right moment

## Voice and Tone: Mailchimp

**Brand character:** The Expert Absurdist

- **Expert:** Ingenuity, skill, resourcefulness, business acumen, precision
- **Absurdist:** Subverting the expected, radical, strange, compelling
- **Together:** Vision + audacity to risk the new + capability to execute

### Voice principles
**Clear communicator:** Concise and direct. When complexity requires it, don't oversimplify.

**Empathetic persuader:** Precise solutions. Empathetic especially around business priorities (we've been a small business too).

**Confident expert:** Makes difficult things actually easy, not just "look easy." Guides and inspires.

**Real person:** Tell it like it is. Concise and believable. Clear human conversation over sly humor.

## Voice and Tone: TurboTax

**Brand character:** Your neighborhood tax visionary — relentlessly in the customer's corner, reimagining taxes with optimism and forward-thinking.

Tone: down to earth, honestly insightful, contagiously confident.

### Voice principles

**Down to earth:** Taxes are intimidating, but we aren't. Put customers at ease. Don't be try-hard with slang.
- Spontaneous: add humor, be current, ground in culture
- Structured: be friendly, simplify, call it what it is

**Honestly insightful:** Radically honest, looking out for customers' best interests. Enable wins with actionable insights. Don't be blunt or navel-gaze.
- Spontaneous: lead with empathy, have their back, say it straight
- Structured: translate jargon, quantify the benefit, offer proactive solutions

**Contagiously confident:** Experts assuring customers they're in good hands. Don't be condescending or over-use exclamation marks.
- Spontaneous: have swagger, be at ease, tell audacious stories
- Structured: speak with authority, back up claims, give customers control

## Accessibility and Inclusion: Readability

**Target:** 5th to 8th grade reading level (10–14 years old).

Why: More inclusive for neurodivergent people, easier for non-native English speakers, better for distracted/tired users, easier to translate.

### Shorter sentences and words
- Short content for small screens, same quality for large screens
- Use shorter, everyday words (use vs. utilize)
- Studies: people skip longer words (8–9 letters) more than shorter ones (3–5 letters)

### Familiar words
Choose familiar, conversational words over complex/jargon — even for complex financial topics. Exception: Use appropriate terminology with professional audiences (accountants).

### Simpler tenses
- to save vs. to be saving
- you import vs. you'll be importing
- you got vs. you had been getting

### Readability scores
Tools: Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level, Gunning Fog Index, Flesch Reading Ease (higher = better). Writer has these built in. Use tools as a guide, not a formula.

## Talking to Customers: Celebrations

Celebrate only genuine customer accomplishments. Used sparingly — too often becomes annoying or condescending.

### When to celebrate (customer-focused milestones)
- Customer reached a self-set goal (paid off credit card, reached savings goal)
- Major milestone (business anniversary, completed taxes, sales milestone)
- Important outcome (refund deposited, high credit score)
- Changed a key habit (more savings, less debt)
- Received a promised payoff (first payroll run, first payment deposited)

### When NOT to celebrate
- Something we did, not they did
- Minor completed tasks (turned on a preference, made progress)
- Events that might have painful context (tax break due to medical expenses, etc.)

### Celebration vs. success message
**Celebrate:** Paid off loan, payment received, loan approved, reached savings goal, first payroll run

**Success message (neutral, friendly):** Turned on mileage tracking, sent an invoice, submitted loan request, categorized a trip, created a budget

Examples of good celebration copy:
- "Monique, you paid off your loan! Fantastic work!"
- "You did it! You just e-filed your taxes."
- "You crushed it! Congrats on paying down your $6,420 credit card debt."

Examples to avoid:
- "Right on! You've signed in successfully." (too minor)
- "Woo hoo! You sent your 57th invoice." (routine task)
- "Nice job. You made it through Step 2!" (patronizing)

## Word List (Key Terms)

Full word list from websitecontentdesign.intuit.com/word-list. Selected entries:

- **401(k)** — not 401K or 401k
- **a.m./p.m.** — use AM/PM (no periods, capitalize)
- **above/below** — avoid (not accessible). Name the exact element or step instead.
- **accept vs. add** — in QuickBooks transaction context, "add" is clearer than "accept"
- **account** — not a proper noun, use lowercase (except "Intuit Account")
- **accounts payable (AP)** — spell out first, lead with plain explanation
- **accounts receivable (AR)** — same
- **ACH** — use "bank transfer" or "bank transfer (ACH)" for clarity. U.S. only.
- **sign in (v.)** / **sign-in (n./adj.)** — not "log in"
- **sign in to** — two words + "to," not "sign into"
- **sign out** — pair with sign in. Never "sign in / log out"
- **sign up (v.)** / **sign-up (n./adj.)** — use for inviting customers to start; "create an account" on sign-in pages
- **simple/simply** — avoid. Simplicity is subjective; what's simple for us may be complex for users.
- **since vs. because** — use "because" for cause and effect; "since" is for time
- **Social Security number, SSN** — spell out and capitalize; SSN only in space-constrained contexts
- **sorry** — apologize only when we've genuinely failed. Don't use as filler.

## Style & Usage: Punctuation (Selected)

Full guidelines at websitecontentdesign.intuit.com/formatting

- **Ampersands** — don't use in body text unless part of a proper noun. OK in navigation, tweets, table headers.
- **Apostrophes** — contractions and possessives. Write like we talk. Avoid regional contractions (y'all, ain't, shan't).
- **Asterisks** — slow readers down. Use sparingly. Goes at end of sentence, before a dash but after other punctuation. 1 asterisk for promo/offer, 2 for features/product info.
- **Brackets** — angle brackets `< >` never used to show UI paths (write out with "select" instead). Square brackets `[ ]` for insertions in quotes.
- **Colons** — use to introduce a list when introductory text is a complete sentence.
- **Serial comma** — Intuit prefers it (AP style deviation).
